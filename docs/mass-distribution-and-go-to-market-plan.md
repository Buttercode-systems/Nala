# Nala Mass-Distribution and Go-to-Market Plan

## Executive position

Nala should become **cell-scalable before nationally open**.

The route to mass distribution is:

`concentrate → package → operate → measure → repeat → automate → expand`

Nala is not only a marketplace where businesses post tasks. It is an operating system that finds neglected small-business work, converts it into fair structured opportunities, prepares workers, verifies outcomes and moves people toward better work.

## Vision

Nala is the trusted bridge that turns “no experience” into verifiable, paid first work for young South Africans while giving small businesses reliable, fairly priced operational support.

## Core loop

`practice → paid work → business verification → portable proof → next opportunity`

The platform must protect this loop while reducing five major risks:

1. insufficient real task supply;
2. a narrow starter catalogue being mistaken for the total vision;
3. workers encountering empty states;
4. monetisation remaining theoretical;
5. progression becoming visually or operationally separate from the core product.

## Launch model: managed market cells

Nala should launch through controlled market cells rather than broad public acquisition.

Each cell should contain:

- one geographic area;
- one or two business verticals;
- 10–20 active businesses;
- 50–100 workers maximum;
- 8–15 approved task products;
- one operations owner;
- one controlled payment process;
- one weekly liquidity review.

### Recommended first cells

1. Gauteng salons and beauty businesses.
2. Gauteng trades, repairs and installation businesses.

The first pilot should not expand geography and task categories simultaneously.

## Target segments

### Workers

- 18–30-year-old first-time workers;
- unemployed or underemployed South Africans;
- mobile-first users with matric, basic digital skills or informal experience;
- township and rural youth reached through trusted cohort partners.

### Businesses

- salons and beauty businesses;
- trades, repairs and installation businesses;
- independent retailers;
- small professional-service firms;
- later: e-commerce, basic technology support and stock operations.

Nala should not initially support hazardous work, delivery, unsupervised care work, cash collection or regulated professional services.

## Task supply engine

Businesses should not need to invent micro-tasks from scratch. Nala should offer outcome-based task products.

### Salon and beauty task products

- Confirm tomorrow’s appointments.
- Follow up cancelled bookings.
- Re-engage dormant customers.
- Clean customer contact lists.
- Prepare review-request messages.
- Prepare approved social captions.
- Update retail-product stock sheets.

### Trades and repairs task products

- Follow up outstanding quotations.
- Confirm installation appointments.
- Prepare payment reminder messages.
- Clean supplier and customer records.
- Request missing job information.
- Prepare after-service review requests.

### Retail and stock task products

- Capture stock counts.
- Clean supplier price lists.
- Identify stock variances.
- Prepare product catalogues.
- Update reorder lists.
- Prepare customer notifications.

Every template must define:

- fixed scope;
- required source inputs;
- expected duration;
- fair-payment floor;
- prohibited-data rules;
- readiness simulation;
- worker checklist;
- evidence requirements;
- review rubric;
- revision limit.

## Phased go-to-market

### Phase 1 — Validate and seed

- Operate one Gauteng market cell.
- Recruit 10–20 active businesses manually.
- Cap workers at 50–100 until liquidity is healthy.
- Launch 10–15 task products.
- Use community organisations, TVETs, youth programmes and WhatsApp groups for controlled worker cohorts.
- Use bookkeepers, business associations, suppliers and incubators for concentrated business demand.
- Test completion fees and managed-service pricing.

### Phase 2 — Grow supply and retention

- Add recurring task plans.
- Add preferred-worker rebooking.
- Add basic tech support, e-commerce catalogue support and retail operations as adjacent categories.
- Introduce partner cohorts only after the first cell meets quality and liquidity gates.
- Add WhatsApp intake and notifications while Nala remains the system of record.

### Phase 3 — Replicate proven cells

- Replicate the same operating playbook into another geography or partner channel.
- Preserve the same task templates, safety rules, pricing logic and market-health thresholds.
- Add regulated payment integrations only after commercial and operational validation.

### Phase 4 — National scale

- Expand through multiple healthy cells.
- Add sponsor and enterprise dashboards.
- Add selected language and accessibility support.
- Evaluate USSD only for narrow status functions, not the full task journey.

## Monetisation

### Workers

Free. Workers should not pay registration, application, simulation, Passport or task-access fees.

### Businesses

Test three models:

1. **Completion fee** — transparent service fee on successfully completed work.
2. **Recurring workflow subscription** — saved templates, recurring scheduling, preferred workers, reporting and higher limits.
3. **Managed service** — Nala packages and operates workflows for a setup, management and per-task fee.

### Sponsored pathways

Partners may fund worker cohorts, task pools, data support or geographic programmes. Sponsorship should accelerate market creation, not replace commercial demand.

## Worker momentum and empty states

Nala must distinguish between:

- suitable work available;
- work exists but readiness is incomplete;
- suitable work is temporarily full;
- no current demand in the worker’s area or category.

Every worker should have one productive next step:

1. complete profile;
2. select work interests;
3. complete baseline capability assessment;
4. pass a readiness simulation;
5. improve a rubric area;
6. create a Work Passport;
7. join a category waitlist;
8. respond to a task notification;
9. complete work;
10. unlock progression.

Nala must never show fabricated marketplace activity or unverified social proof.

## North-star metric

**Verified repeat work relationships**

A business completes and pays for a second task with the same worker or task family within 60 days.

Supporting metrics:

- active businesses posting per month;
- recurring task share;
- task fill rate;
- median time to match;
- qualified workers per task;
- first-pass approval rate;
- revision and dispute rates;
- effective worker hourly earnings;
- repeat-paying businesses;
- contribution margin per task family;
- worker progression to higher-value work.

## Distribution-readiness gates

### Product

- complete worker and business lifecycle without manual database editing;
- clear waitlist and empty states;
- persisted progression data;
- tested low-data behaviour;
- accessible mobile experience.

### Supply

- 25 active recurring businesses;
- 200 funded task completions;
- at least 40% recurring tasks;
- at least three healthy task families;
- no single business above 20% of task volume.

### Worker quality

- at least 70% first-pass approval;
- fair effective earnings measured;
- dropout reasons understood;
- worker satisfaction measured.

### Commercial

- at least 10 paying businesses;
- at least five repeat-paying businesses;
- one pricing model showing repeat acceptance;
- positive margin on recurring task products.

### Technical and trust

- production Supabase schema and RLS;
- environment isolation;
- feature flags;
- audit logs;
- error monitoring;
- backups and rollback;
- verified business process;
- dispute and safety workflows;
- payment-state reconciliation.

## Credibility rules

Do not claim:

- a live FNB integration without approval;
- a wallet, escrow or lending product;
- guaranteed earnings or employment;
- national impact before validated deployment;
- fake users, payouts or partner relationships;
- care, delivery, USSD or broad marketplace functionality before implementation.

## Final operating principle

Nala becomes mass-distribution ready by becoming **cell-scalable**:

- concentrate demand;
- package repeatable work;
- control worker capacity;
- measure fair outcomes;
- prove repeat usage and willingness to pay;
- automate only after the manual operating pattern is understood;
- replicate one healthy cell at a time.
