# Nala Pitch Deck and Partner Brief

## Positioning

**Nala — First real work, done right.**

Nala turns the experience trap into a controlled progression journey:

`practice → paid starter work → business verification → portable proof → next opportunity`

Workers do not pay for access. Businesses and approved partners fund useful work and the operating infrastructure around it.

---

## Pitch deck: 12-slide narrative

### Slide 1 — Cover

**Nala**

**First real work, done right.**

Turning “no experience” into verified opportunity while helping small businesses complete useful operational work.

### Slide 2 — The problem

South African young people face a circular barrier:

- employers ask for experience;
- first-time workers cannot gain experience without being hired;
- conventional training often ends without paid work or employer-verified evidence.

At the same time, small businesses carry recurring operational backlogs:

- appointments go unconfirmed;
- quotations are not followed up;
- customer and stock records become messy;
- routine content and reminders are delayed;
- the work is too small for a permanent hire but still matters.

Existing alternatives are frequently fragmented, informal or weak on worker protection, task clarity and proof.

### Slide 3 — The solution

Nala converts neglected business work into fair, structured starter opportunities.

Workers receive:

- realistic readiness simulations;
- transparent task scope and payment;
- guided work delivery;
- business verification;
- a portable Work Passport;
- evidence-based progression recommendations.

Businesses receive:

- approved outcome-based task products;
- task packaging and safety checks;
- fair-pay guidance;
- controlled worker preparation;
- simple review and revision boundaries;
- recurring plans and preferred-worker pathways.

### Slide 4 — Product

#### Worker journey

1. Explore freely without being forced to sign in.
2. View live market availability and preparation pathways.
3. Practise a matching work situation.
4. Accept a transparent, funded task when eligible.
5. Complete work through a guided workbench.
6. Receive business verification.
7. Add evidence to the Work Passport.
8. Follow a clear next step.

#### Business journey

1. Describe an operational outcome publicly without creating an account.
2. Use the task-packaging assistant to map the need to an approved product.
3. Review required inputs, boundaries and fair-pay guidance.
4. Complete verification and payment-readiness gates.
5. Fund and publish the task through the controlled workflow.
6. Review delivered work.
7. Rebook a preferred worker or create a recurring plan.

#### Operations and partner journey

- monitor market-cell liquidity;
- control worker capacity;
- review task supply and repeat work;
- plan funded cohorts;
- track real, privacy-safe outcomes.

### Slide 5 — Current state

Nala currently has:

- a live production web application;
- worker, business and operations experiences;
- a Practice Library and readiness simulations;
- a guided task lifecycle;
- Work Passport and progression modules;
- live Supabase market-cell and task-product foundations;
- public market-health and availability views;
- structured business intake;
- worker category-interest capture;
- recurring-task and fair-pay planning;
- task-packaging assistance;
- trust, safety and distribution-control foundations.

Do not describe demo journeys, seeded task products or design validation as real marketplace traction. Real user, task, earnings and repeat-business metrics should be added only after pilots produce them.

### Slide 6 — Market opportunity

Nala addresses two large structural needs:

1. first-time workers need credible paid experience, not only another job listing or certificate;
2. small businesses need affordable help with repetitive operational work that does not justify a full-time hire.

The initial commercial wedge is deliberately focused:

- salons and beauty businesses;
- trades, repairs and installation businesses;
- adjacent retail, e-commerce and basic technology support.

The long-term opportunity is a network of repeatable market cells that can expand by geography, partner and task family.

### Slide 7 — Business model

#### Workers

Free access to:

- opportunity browsing;
- readiness simulations;
- task access;
- Work Passport;
- progression guidance.

#### Businesses

Revenue experiments:

1. transparent completion fee on successfully completed tasks;
2. recurring workflow subscription for saved templates, repeat scheduling, reporting and higher limits;
3. managed-service fees for task packaging, operations and partner programmes.

#### Partners

- funded worker cohorts;
- sponsored task pools;
- enterprise and supplier-development programmes;
- partner reporting and controlled distribution infrastructure.

Nala does not currently claim a wallet, escrow, lending product or live banking integration.

### Slide 8 — Competitive advantage

Nala is not another job board and not another certificate platform.

Its advantage is the closed loop:

`real business demand → structured task → prepared worker → verified completion → portable proof → progression`

Defensible assets include:

- a growing library of approved task products;
- fair-pay and safety rules;
- readiness simulations mapped to real work;
- business and worker trust data;
- repeat-work relationships;
- market-cell liquidity controls;
- verified Proof-of-Work records;
- operating playbooks for partner-led expansion.

### Slide 9 — Go-to-market

Nala expands through managed market cells rather than uncontrolled national acquisition.

#### First cell

- Gauteng;
- salons/beauty and trades/repairs;
- 10–20 active businesses;
- 50–100 workers maximum;
- 8–15 approved task products;
- weekly liquidity and fair-pay review.

#### Distribution channels

Worker side:

- TVETs;
- youth programmes;
- NGOs and community organisations;
- churches and trusted WhatsApp communities;
- employer-funded cohorts.

Business side:

- accountants and bookkeepers;
- suppliers and franchise groups;
- business incubators and chambers;
- enterprise-development programmes;
- approved fintech and banking partners later.

### Slide 10 — Team

Include:

- founder name and role;
- direct experience relevant to South African workers, small businesses, operations or product development;
- technical contributors;
- operations, labour, legal, financial-services or youth-employment advisors only when confirmed.

Do not present informal conversations or unconfirmed supporters as formal advisors.

### Slide 11 — Ask and use of funds

The funding ask should be set after a bottom-up operating model is complete.

Primary uses:

- business-demand acquisition;
- task-product design and moderation;
- worker and business operations;
- trust and safety;
- regulated payment integration;
- pilot data and impact measurement;
- engineering and reliability;
- controlled partner distribution.

The ask should be tied to concrete pilot milestones rather than broad national marketing.

### Slide 12 — Vision and close

**Every first chance should lead somewhere.**

Nala’s long-term vision is a South Africa where:

- young people can create credible first work experience;
- useful small-business work becomes accessible opportunity;
- workers carry proof that belongs to them;
- businesses discover reliable future talent;
- economic participation grows through fair, repeatable work.

## One-page partner brief

### The problem

Young South Africans are blocked by the experience trap, while small businesses leave useful operational work unfinished because it is too small or irregular for another permanent employee.

### The Nala model

Nala packages neglected business needs into controlled paid tasks. Workers practise first, complete the work with clear boundaries, receive business verification and build a portable Work Passport. Verified evidence then guides the next opportunity.

### Why a managed network

Nala does not recruit unlimited workers into an empty marketplace. It builds controlled market cells around funded task supply, repeat businesses, fair pay and measurable quality.

### Partner opportunities

Partners can support:

- business-demand aggregation;
- funded task pools;
- controlled worker cohorts;
- training-to-work pathways;
- geographic pilots;
- data, transport or equipment support after approval;
- progression into larger opportunities.

### What Nala measures

- funded tasks completed;
- first-pass approval;
- worker earnings and effective hourly pay;
- repeat-paying businesses;
- recurring task share;
- repeat worker-business relationships;
- dispute and safety rates;
- progression into higher-value work.

### Current boundaries

Nala does not claim:

- guaranteed work or earnings;
- national impact before pilots;
- live FNB or banking integration;
- a regulated wallet or escrow product;
- unsupported delivery, care or hazardous-work categories.

### Partnership principle

Fund real work first. Recruit workers in line with credible demand. Expand one healthy market cell at a time.

## Financial model framework

Create monthly projections using these drivers:

### Demand

- active businesses;
- tasks per active business;
- recurring-task percentage;
- average task value;
- completion rate;
- repeat-business rate.

### Revenue

- service-fee percentage;
- minimum service fee;
- subscription accounts and monthly price;
- managed-service contracts;
- sponsored programme revenue.

### Worker economics

- average worker pay per task;
- estimated active and revision minutes;
- effective hourly pay;
- payment delay;
- worker repeat rate.

### Operating costs

- business acquisition;
- worker support;
- task moderation;
- payment processing;
- trust and safety;
- technology;
- partner reporting;
- refunds and disputes.

### Core outputs

- gross task value;
- Nala revenue;
- contribution margin per task family;
- business CAC and payback;
- active-worker utilisation;
- monthly burn and runway;
- break-even task volume.

Use conservative scenarios until real pilot data replaces assumptions.
