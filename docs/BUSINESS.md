# Business Model and Go-to-Market

## Economic customer

Workers receive the value but should not be the primary paying customer.

Initial paying or funding customers:
- small businesses buying completed task outcomes;
- enterprises sponsoring youth-work cohorts;
- NGOs and foundations funding employment outcomes;
- employers accessing verified emerging talent;
- training providers seeking work-linked progression evidence.

## Revenue model

### MVP

Business pays:
- disclosed task amount for the worker;
- separate Nala service fee for task packaging, matching, quality controls and support.

The task amount and platform fee must be displayed separately.

### Later

- Workflow subscription for repeat business users.
- Sponsored cohort contracts.
- Verified talent access or successful-placement fees, subject to fair recruitment practice.
- Programme dashboards and outcome reporting.
- Payment-partner revenue only where transparent and appropriate.

## Supply strategy

Do not begin with an empty open marketplace.

Seed demand through fixed outcomes:
- reduce appointment no-shows;
- follow up unanswered quotations;
- prepare overdue-invoice reminders;
- clean contact or stock spreadsheets;
- prepare basic promotional content.

Nala should help the business recognise and package neglected work rather than expecting it to write a freelance brief.

## Initial market wedge

Service businesses with simple, repeated customer administration:
- salons and barbers;
- cleaning and maintenance services;
- tradespeople and installers;
- small clinics only for non-clinical, privacy-safe workflows;
- tutors and training businesses;
- small retailers and hospitality businesses.

## Acquisition

### Businesses
- founder-led outreach;
- industry associations and local business networks;
- accountants, bookkeepers and business-support organisations;
- sponsored pilot with a defined task outcome;
- case studies focused on time saved and revenue recovered.

### Workers
- youth organisations;
- TVETs and training providers;
- community programmes;
- referral after task supply is available;
- mobile-first social campaigns tied to real opportunities, not waitlists.

## Marketplace controls

Track supply and demand per task category and geography. Pause worker acquisition where paid demand is insufficient. Do not market guaranteed income.

## Unit economics to validate

- Average task value.
- Nala fee per completed task.
- AI and support cost per task.
- Payment processing cost.
- Revision and dispute cost.
- Business acquisition cost.
- Worker onboarding-to-completion conversion.
- Repeat task rate.
- Contribution margin per completed task.

## Partnership stance

Use accurate language:
- “designed for integration” when no agreement exists;
- “pilot participant” only with consent;
- “verified partner” only with a documented relationship.

FNB, government, employers or funders must never be presented as partners merely because Nala is entering a challenge or using compatible infrastructure.

## Defensibility

Nala's long-term advantage comes from:
- task-template and outcome data;
- safe workflow packaging;
- verified performance records;
- progression outcomes;
- trusted business and worker network;
- operational expertise in first-work success;
- evidence standards portable across employers.

The moat is not the chatbot or CV generator.
